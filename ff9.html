Final Fantasy IX

Released in 2000 for the original PlayStation, Final Fantasy IX takes a heartfelt leap back to the fantasy roots of the series. You play as Zidane, a cheeky thief with a tail, who ends up entangled in a world-saving quest alongside one of the most memorable casts in RPG history.

The game blends whimsical charm with some seriously emotional storytelling. From airships and kingdoms to identity crises and epic summons, FFIX hits all the JRPG notes just right. The ATB combat system is classic, and the art direction? Straight-up storybook magic.

It's a love letter to fans of the older Final Fantasy games, with plenty of references, callbacks, and heart. If you ever wanted to go on a grand adventure with a knight who says “you spoony bard,” this is it.

A Veteran's Review

As someone who’s been gaming since floppy disks and CRT fuzz, I can say with a warm smirk that *Final Fantasy IX* is more than a game—it’s a gentle reminder of why we fell in love with RPGs in the first place.

After the edge and angst of *FF7* and *FF8*, Square made a bold move. Instead of chasing trends, *FFIX* circled back to its fantasy roots with castles, mages, dragons, and a whole lot of heart. This wasn’t regression it was a love letter. Led by Hironobu Sakaguchi himself, this game was a “thank you” to fans who stuck with the series from the pixel days.

The art is whimsical. The characters? Exaggerated in all the right ways. Sure, they don’t brood in leather jackets or wield oversized swords but they feel alive, sincere. The visuals feel like a bedtime story drawn by someone who never forgot what wonder looks like.

The gameplay embraces clarity over chaos. Every party member has a role—you’re not duct-taping Materia or gambling with junctions. And while that may sound “limited,” it’s actually freeing. It’s satisfying to let Steiner be a clunky knight and Vivi be a classic black mage with fireballs and charm in equal measure.

And the story? No spoilers, but let’s just say it hits different when you're older. It’s light on cynicism and heavy on soul. There are lessons here—quiet ones about legacy, identity, even mortality but never shoved in your face. It’s got just enough satire to keep you smiling and just enough sincerity to hit your gut when you least expect it.

*Final Fantasy IX* didn’t try to be cool. It tried to be good. And it succeeded.

“A classic not because it reinvented the wheel, but because it reminded us why we built the wheel in the first place.”
